The problem

The problem isn't the agents. It's what you'd have to give them.

Your company's knowledge lives in a dozen places: the ERP, the CRM, email threads, chat messages, spreadsheets, shared drives. An agent doing real finance work needs to read across all of them. Hand over broad access and you have created a risk no auditor will sign off on. Hold everything back and the agent is useless.

Money makes it sharper. An agent that pays invoices or chases receivables touches cash. One bad call on a convincing fake invoice and the money is gone before anyone looks.

The big vendors sell agent governance, but only inside their own products, at their prices, watching only the data that lives in their systems. Your business does not run inside one product.

What Nien does

Three controls, on every action, from the first day.

Each agent sees only what it needs

Nien connects to the systems you already run and hands each agent a defined slice. The accounts payable agent sees supplier invoices and payment terms. It does not see payroll, contracts, or board minutes. Scope is set per agent, per task, and you can narrow it or cut it at any time.

scope: per agent, per task

Your rules, checked the same way every time

Every action an agent attempts runs through Nomos, the rules engine at the center of Nien. The rules are yours: spend limits, who must approve what, which accounts an agent may touch. The check is not another AI making a judgment call. Same action, same rules, same answer, every time. No model ever decides whether a payment goes out.

no model in the approval path

Agents earn trust, and we keep score

Every action produces a signed record: what the agent tried, what the rules said, what happened. That record becomes the agent's grade. New agents start by watching and suggesting. They act on their own only after the record shows they have earned it, and only inside the limits you set. Third-party agents on the platform are graded exactly the same way.

trust: earned on the record

The Gartner signal

The correction is coming.

40%of enterprises, by 2027

Gartner predicted in May 2026 that 40% of enterprises will demote or decommission autonomous agents by 2027, because governance gaps tend to surface only after an incident. Their recommendation: run agents on tiered autonomy levels, from observe, to advise, to act with approval, to act on their own.

That is a prediction about the market, not a comment on Nien. But it describes exactly the failure Nien exists to prevent, and those tiers are how Nien has worked from the start.
