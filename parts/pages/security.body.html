The controls

Six claims your auditors can test.

No judgment calls on money

An AI can draft an invoice or propose a payment. It cannot approve one. Every action runs through rules your team wrote, and the check works the same way every time. There is no model in the approval path, so there is nothing to be confused, persuaded, or prompted into letting a payment through.

Approvals that match your controls

If your policy says payments above a threshold need two sets of eyes, Nien enforces it. Named approvers, quorums, separate rules per entity or department. An agent cannot approve its own work, and an approval that skips the required people is not slow or discouraged. It is impossible.

Revocation in one step

Suspend an agent and it stops mid-task. Its data access ends at the same moment, not at the next sync. There is no window where a suspended agent can still read or act. Restoring it is a deliberate decision by a person with the authority to make it.

A record no one can quietly edit

Every action carries a signed, timestamped entry: the data the agent read, the action it attempted, the rule that allowed or blocked it, and who approved. Records are tamper-evident, so a changed entry is a visible entry. Exports map to your existing audit process.

Autonomy is earned, never assumed

No agent starts with the ability to act alone. It starts by observing, then advising, then acting with approval, and only its accumulated record justifies each step up. This matches the tiered-autonomy approach Gartner now recommends. You can hold any agent at any tier indefinitely.

Identity you already manage

Agents appear in the identity system you already run, such as Microsoft Entra, alongside your staff. The same joiner and leaver process that covers employees covers agents. No new identity silo, no separate set of credentials for your IT team to forget about.
